Why the lowest volume on a Galaxy is still too loud

One UI, like all of Android, splits the range between silence and full output into a fixed number of steps. The lowest step above mute is a fixed fraction of the maximum, not a fixed loudness. Galaxy phones and tablets are tuned for a loud maximum so they sound good in a store or outdoors, which means that first fraction lands loud too. There is no setting anywhere in One UI to move where that floor sits. That is the whole problem, and it is why turning it down as far as the slider goes still is not far enough.

Why Sound Assistant and 1% steps don't fix it

Good Lock → Sound Assistant Doesn't lower the floor

Sound Assistant, from Samsung's Good Lock suite, is the most common suggestion, and it is a genuinely useful tool. It changes the volume increment from roughly 10% to 1%, so you get about 100 steps instead of ten.

But more steps is not the same as a lower floor. The lowest 1% step is still 1% of the same loud maximum, which lands at almost exactly the same loudness as before. It makes the climb from mute smoother; it does not add any level that is quieter than the quietest step you already had. If step one was too loud, a finer step one is still too loud.

Disable Absolute Volume (Bluetooth only) Situational

For Bluetooth output, turning on Developer options and disabling Absolute Volume can change how the steps map between the Galaxy and the headphones. It sometimes helps the steps land at more usable levels over Bluetooth, but it does nothing for the built-in speaker or wired output, and it does not lower the underlying floor.

Galaxy Buds too loud even at one bar

Galaxy Buds, Buds2, Buds2 Pro, and Buds3 all share the phone's media volume over Bluetooth, so they inherit the same problem, made worse by sensitive in-ear drivers sitting directly in your ear canal. Many owners report that even a single bar is too loud for quiet listening or falling asleep. Disabling Absolute Volume can help the mapping, but the reliable way to get below that first bar is to attenuate the media output itself, which is what the fix below does. It works the same whether you are on the buds, wired earphones, or the phone speaker.

Galaxy Tab: often the worst offender

Galaxy Tab speakers and headphone output frequently have an even higher minimum step than phones, because tablets are built to fill a room. If you use a Tab S for late-night video, audiobooks, or white noise, the lowest step can be dramatically too loud. The below-floor method works identically on tablets, and this is usually where the difference is most obvious.

The fix that actually goes below Samsung's floor

Below-floor attenuation through Android's audio engine Actually works

Instead of re-slicing the existing steps, this approach lays a negative-gain audio effect on top of the lowest system step, using Android's own DynamicsProcessing framework. That produces output genuinely quieter than the hardware minimum, because it is not working inside the existing step range at all. It is present on every Galaxy device, so it needs no Good Lock, no Samsung account, and no root.

This is how Granular Volume works: a small floating dial that sits over any app with seven steps, from no change down to a full 30 dB below your hardware minimum. Drag it anywhere, tuck it to an edge, or toggle it from a Quick Settings tile. It does not touch your volume buttons, One UI's volume panel, Knox, or Samsung Pay. It just adds the quiet end Samsung leaves out.

FAQ

Why is the lowest volume on my Samsung Galaxy still too loud?
The first step above mute is a fixed fraction of the maximum, not a fixed loudness, and Galaxy devices are tuned for a loud maximum. One UI has no setting to move where that floor sits.
Does Samsung Sound Assistant lower the minimum volume?
No. It changes the increment to 1% for many more steps, but the lowest step is still the same fraction of the same maximum. It adds steps within the range; it does not extend the range below the floor.
Why are my Galaxy Buds too loud even at one bar?
The Buds share the phone's media volume over Bluetooth, and even the first bar is a fraction of a loud maximum played on sensitive in-ear drivers. Disabling Absolute Volume can help the mapping but does not lower the floor.
Do I need Good Lock or a Samsung account to fix this?
No. The below-floor method uses Android's own audio-effect framework, present on every Galaxy device regardless of Good Lock, Sound Assistant, or a Samsung account.
Does this work on Samsung tablets like the Tab S series?
Yes, and it matters most there. Galaxy Tab output often has an even higher minimum step than phones, so the same attenuation is usually more noticeable.
Does it need root or ADB on a Galaxy device?
No. It needs a display-over-other-apps permission and standard audio permissions. No root, no ADB, nothing that affects Knox, Samsung Pay, or Secure Folder.
Will this affect calls or only media on my Galaxy?
It targets the media output stream. Calls, ringtones, and alarms use separate Android audio streams and are not changed.
Is it free, and does it collect any data?
Free, no ads, no account, no in-app purchases. It has no internet permission at all, so it cannot send data anywhere, and it is open source under GPL-3.0.
